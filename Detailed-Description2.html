PLANS AND PROGRESS

The idea of Magnum Opus was born out of my astronomical love for board games and alchemy. Both subjects are not yet to be popular in Vietnam, alchemy is even more so in the entire world. I want board games to be more than just a community; it is my grand desire to make board games interesting and accessible to everyone, just like how it is overseas. Magnum Opus will be the first step, and alchemy will be its supporting theme. Although alchemy is an obscured knowledge and is considered pseudo-science, it still has a rich history before the world shifted to modern chemistry. Moreover, we still use alchemical terms up until today, and alchemy was still based on intense research which is proof that alchemy is not entirely false. In my opinion, the truth about the subject is worth attention. Through Magnum Opus’ rich and deep storyline, I intend to teach, or at the very least, interest, people about alchemy. I began developing the rule of the game long before I started knowing about the course’s project, aiming Magnum Opus to be one of the most unique card games. It was out of convenience that I used it as the final project for this course.

On the first day of the course project, Magnum Opus was considered complete on the rule book and gameplay. I have refined it further up until now to make the rules simpler. Below are the up-to-date rules of Magnum Opus, although if I must be honest, I am yet to be content with this result. It currently lacks subtlety and interaction between players, which are the most important aspect of a card game.

Magnum Opus is a dueling card game between two genius inventors. In order to win, one must be able to fulfill three given tasks by using the deck they have built. Players will constantly create new cards to match the situation and create advantages for themselves while slowing down the opponent through the infusion of unique elements. At the start of each turn, the player will check if they have fulfilled a task or not, and earn a Sigil if they do. Once a player earns his 3rd Sigil, he is declared the winner.

Each player will have a deck of 30 cards that are built from their card collection. Each player will draw up to 5 cards in their hand at the start of their turn. Players have no card limits on hand. Whenever they run out of cards, they will reshuffle their discard pile, making it the new draw deck

At the start of the turn, the player will choose to gain one mana of one of the 4 elements. If they choose the same mana on the next turn, they will gain one more. There is no maximum to the amount of mana you can hold. On a turn, a player will do the following steps in order:

  1. Mana Infusion: Infuse spell cards with elemental mana
  2. Cast a spell, play artifacts or creatures, and use artifacts and creatures to gain effects

Mana Infusion: Every spell card has a base effect, and when they are infused with an element, their effect is enhanced. When they have been infused with 3 mana, their effect changes completely into something more powerful. A card can only be infused with a maximum of 3 mana.

Sigil: This signifies that the player has fulfilled one of the three given tasks. Whenever a player gains his 3rd Sigil, he is declared the winner.

The three given tasks are:

  • At the start of your turn, have an accumulated total of 20 powers among your creatures in play.
  • Playing 3 spells that contain at least 3 elements.
  • Each turn, the player will put a counter on an artifact. At the start of their turn, when they have 4 counters on an artifact, they may sacrifice to gain a Sigil.

There are three types of cards in this game: creatures, spells, and artifacts.

  • Creatures: Creatures require no mana to play, but they cannot be used in the turn they are summoned. Each creature has a stat as their health points. When they run out of health points, they are sent to the discard pile. Each creature can be used to perform one action per turn by using mana, having different effects depending on which element you used. Some creatures have a Play effect that is resolved when they are played from your hand.
    • Players can only have a maximum of three creatures on their board.
  • Spells: Spells are single-use cards. When you play a Spell card, resolve its effect then put it in the discard pile.
  • Artifacts: Artifacts are like creatures, except that they do not have stats. This also means they cannot be dealt damage; they can only be destroyed through the effect of a card. Artifacts can only be used several times before they break. When they do, they are sent to the discard pile.
    • Players can only have a maximum of two artifacts on their board.

The theme of the game is to be set in “futuristic medieval”, a modern world where pseudoscience is science. The main color palette of the UI/UX will be colors of the metal, ranging from bronze to platinum, with detailed glimmers and shadows to add the feeling of mystery. Metal colors depict a stern, meticulous, and flawless design, suggesting that the game is highly competitive and requires immense focus to achieve victory. Cards will have a design of an ancient scribe that is worn out, and players can use a type of in-game resource to upgrade the design to a metallic finish. Aside from being a commercial aesthetic aspect, it creates a sense of achievement when players have played long enough to get a better version of something they have been using

The login screen will first picture an alchemist working in his dimly lit laboratory that is equipped with an intricate system of alchemical mechanism, with vials of potion displayed disorderly. These will be 3D animation to create a more realistic atmosphere, such as the alchemist moving around the laboratory, and the potion boiling in the machine. This is to first welcome new players and hint that they will be experiencing a game that stresses creativity and memory. As the players progress further and unlock more classes, they can change their login screen into the laboratory of their class of choice.

The menu of the game will also be on the login screen, with the Play button right in the middle bottom and other basic tabs such as card library, art gallery, or minigames placed on bookshelves. When players enter Play, there will be tabs to display several modes to play the game: the classic unranked/ranked, puzzles, research, and others that are under development.

Unranked/Ranked will follow the standard rules. Ranked will be divided into several tiers; only players of the same tier can match each other in Ranked. At the end of a season, players will get rewards depending on their tier.

Puzzles are scenarios for players to practice decision making and learn how the cards interact with each other. However, Magnum Opus will have a unique puzzle: a standardized test to train the players’ memory on the combinations of elements for spell cards. This is extra useful as I predict that players will have a hard time at first to familiarize themselves with this unique system of Mana Infusion.

Research also follows the standard rules, but you won’t be bringing your pre-built deck. Rather, you will build the deck with randomly given cards, and you will compete with players that did the same. This mode can improve players’ creativity and introduce new strategies as you will have to play accordingly to the cards you get.
